English workshop | Q 8 | Page 5

Read the poem. Write an appreciation of the poem in about 12-15 lines with the help of the given points.

  • Title
  • Poet
  • Rhyme scheme
  • Favourite line
  • Theme/Central idea
  • Figures of speech
  • Special features - Type of the poem, language, tone, implied meaning, etc.
  • Why I like/ dislike the poem

Solution


‘Where the Mind is Without Fear’ is an excellent poem of ‘Naivedya’ a collection of poems. It is a free English translation of Rabindranath’s Bengali poem, ‘Chitta Jetha Bhayshunya’. The translation was done by the poet himself.

In this poem, the poet breathes a prayer that comes out from the depth of his heart. The poet is a preacher of freedom of every sort. He pleads for the spiritual emancipation of his own motherland. He prays for the uplift of his countrymen, wants them to reside in heaven where the mind is free from all sorts of narrowness, the head is held high with full dignity achieved through pure knowledge and relentless strivings for perfection. The poet does not pray for his own personal gains. His prayer is sincere and imbibed with lyrical emotion chanted with full-throated ease.

Rabindranath composed this sonnet only in one sentence. The structure of the Sonnet is suitable for expressing the theme of the poem. The poem has been divided into three parts (8 + 4 + 2). The poem has no specific pattern of the rhyming scheme because it is free verse. The poem comprises several figures of speech like metaphor, personification, repetition, alliteration. The poet conveys strong feelings for the progress of the country; an extremely patriotic poem. The line “Where the world has not been broken into fragments, By narrow domestic walls” is one of my favourite. In this line, he says the world has been divided into several fragments due to differences and disputes (which he refers to as narrow, domestic walls).  He wants people to rise above these differences. I like the poem as it is deeply sensitive, fresh, and beautiful.

  3. Introduction of the topic

  5. Conclusion

Solution


Dream India

Respected Principal, teachers, and my dear friends, today, 15th August, is the day India won her freedom. No doubt, at that time the citizens of free India had many dreams for their country, some of which would have certainly come true. I, Rohan Mehta, often think about my dream India too, and I would like to tell you something about it.

Read the short story written by Rabindranath Tagore ‘The Kabuliwala’ or ‘The Home Coming.’


Solution


The Kabuliwala


The Kabuliwala by Rabindranath Tagore is a poignant short story set in Calcutta (now Kolkata), India. The story revolves around the relationship between a little girl named Mini and a Kabuliwala (a fruit seller from Kabul, Afghanistan) named Rahamat. The Kabuliwala, who has left his homeland and family to make a living in India, befriends Mini and sees a striking resemblance between her and his own daughter back in Kabul.


As the story unfolds, Mini's innocent and compassionate nature draws her towards Rahamat, and they share a warm bond. The Kabuliwala becomes a regular visitor to Mini's house, bringing her nuts and dried fruits.

The story explores the themes of cross-cultural connections, empathy, and the way human relationships can bridge geographical and cultural gaps.


However, as time passes, circumstances change. Rahamat is arrested for stabbing a customer during a dispute. After serving his sentence, he returns to Mini's neighborhood, but much has changed. Mini has grown up, and her father is now cautious about her interactions with Rahamat, fearing the worst. The emotional climax of the story occurs when Rahamat visits Mini after his release, and she struggles to recognize him. Her father intervenes and makes her realize the passage of time and the changes that have occurred during her childhood.


The Kabuliwala highlights the ephemeral nature of relationships, the effects of time and separation, and the complexities of human emotions. It brings out the universal truth that while time may alter appearances and situations, the bonds of genuine affection remain etched in the hearts of those who share them.




The Homecoming


The Homecoming by Rabindranath Tagore is a poignant and heartwarming story that revolves around the theme of reunion and emotional reconnection. The narrative follows the protagonist, Upen, who has been living far away from his home and family for many years due to his work. Upon hearing about his father's illness, Upen decides to return to his village, rekindling his sense of belonging and attachment to his roots.


As Upen makes his way back home, he reminisces about his childhood and the fond memories associated with his family and village. The story beautifully captures the emotional journey of rediscovering one's identity and the significance of familial bonds. Upon arriving home, Upen is met with a mix of emotions – the joy of reuniting with his loved ones and the realization that time has changed many things.


Through Upen's interactions with his family members and the village, Tagore highlights the essence of cultural heritage, tradition, and the impact of urbanization on rural life. Upen's return prompts reflection on the changing dynamics within his family and the village community.


The story The Homecoming delves into themes of nostalgia, the passage of time, and the importance of staying connected to one's roots. Tagore's lyrical prose captures the emotions of both the protagonist and the environment, painting a vivid picture of the homecoming experience. The story culminates in a bittersweet moment as Upen realizes that while certain things have changed irreversibly, the love and sense of belonging he feels remain eternal.
